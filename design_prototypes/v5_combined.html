The experiment

Brief deliberately inverted: instead of capping HR and watching power, hold ~150 W at ≥90 rpm and watch what HR does in a hot room (82 °F, ceiling fan only), time-boxed to ~30 minutes. Execution matched intent — 52% of the ride (17.3 min) sat inside 140–160 W, cadence held 88–91 in every block, one finishing sprint (427 W), short cooldown. A controlled test, not a class ridden hot.

Because power and cadence were both flat while HR rose 21 bpm, the drift can't be attributed to either — it's pure thermal load. The ride started exactly where outdoor Memphis rides sit at 150 W (HR 136–142) and then heat storage took over: no steady state was ever reached.

The findings

  • Airflow, not air temperature, is the dominant variable. 82 °F of still air produced more drift in 30 minutes than the 95 °F outdoor ride on 6/28 produced per hour (+7 bpm over 3.1 h). Moving at 14 mph outdoors is what makes evaporative cooling work; a ceiling fan doesn't come close.
  • Peloton calibration: its meter read 163 W avg / 320 kJ against the Assiomas' 138 W / 277 kJ — ~15–18% high. All comparisons use pedal numbers from here.
  • Cadence transfer: the 7/17 leg-speed gains held indoors (median 89, 89% of pedaling ≥85) — and at matched power the 90 rpm target cost ~nothing, consistent with the standing finding (~0.3 bpm per 10 rpm).

Next

Two rules for all future indoor work: (1) a strong direct fan (blower at torso height) is the prerequisite for HR-capped indoor Z2 — under a ceiling fan, "HR ≤138" caps power near ~120 W by minute 15 and the session trains almost nothing; (2) until the fan exists, pace indoor rides by power (Z2 = 130–170 W on the pedals), talk-test as backstop, and treat indoor HR as thermally inflated past minute ~10.
